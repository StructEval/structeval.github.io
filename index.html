Introduction

We introduce StructEval, a benchmark for evaluating LLMs' abilities to produce structured outputs across a wide range of data formats and task settings. Unlike prior benchmarks that target plain text generation, StructEval focuses on tasks involving both generation of and conversion between structured data formats, such as JSON, HTML, React, LaTeX, and CSV. It covers a total of 44 tasks grouped into two primary paradigms: structured generation and structured conversion. The dataset includes 2,035 carefully validated instances, annotated with high precision and designed to test reasoning, formatting, and representation fidelity in LLM outputs.

Introduction & Motivation

In recent years, there has been a significant surge in the capabilities of large language models (LLMs) in generating human-like text and performing a wide range of natural language processing tasks. State-of-the-art models like GPT-4o, OpenAI o1/o3, and Google's Gemini have achieved superior performance in knowledge QA, instruction-following, and code generation.

Despite recent advances, many real-world applications require not only fluency in the content of the output but also precise control over its structure. This includes tasks where the expected output must follow specific formats such as JSON, XML, LaTeX, HTML, or code in frameworks like React or Vue. These types of structured output are essential in domains like software development, data pipelines, user interface generation, and scientific publishing, where incorrect formatting can lead to disrupted pipelines or non-functional outputs.

However, most existing benchmarks focus on the semantic quality or reasoning ability of LLMs, with limited emphasis on their ability to produce format-conforming structured outputs. Some recently proposed benchmarks aim to evaluate the quality of structured outputs but tend to target specific modalities, such as code generation or text-only structures, rather than offering comprehensive evaluations across diverse structured formats. As existing benchmarks gradually become more saturated, it is still unknown how the current state-of-the-art models perform in structured generation tasks.

To address these challenges, we introduce StructEval, a comprehensive benchmark that systematically evaluates LLMs' abilities to produce highly structured output. Our benchmark encompasses 21 distinct formats and 44 task types organized into two complementary subsets: StructEval-T (text-only structures) and StructEval-V (visually rendered outputs). Both subsets include generation and conversion tasks. We have developed a novel assessment framework that integrates syntactic validity checking, keyword matching, and visual question answering, providing a holistic measure of both structural correctness and output fidelity.

StructEval evaluates the LLM's capability to generate structured outputs, including text-only tasks like JSON, TOML, etc, and visual rendering tasks like HTML, React, Latex, etc.

Experiment Results

Leaderboard

We evaluate various models including LLMs and LMMs. In each type, we consider both closed- and open-source models. Our evaluation is conducted under a zero-shot setting to assess the capability of models to generate accurate answers without fine-tuning or few-shot demonstrations on our benchmark. For all models, we use the default prompt provided by each model for multi-choice or open QA, if available. If models do not provide prompts for task types in StructEval, we conduct prompt engineering on the validation set and use the most effective prompt for the later zero-shot experiment.

Different Format Types

We compare the performance of various models across the most frequent structured output formats. Across all types, GPT-4 and Claude consistently outperform other models by a significant margin. Open-source models demonstrate relatively strong performance in categories like JSON and YAML, which are more common formats. However, for more specialized formats like SVG, LaTeX equations, and React components, all models except the leading proprietary ones obtain lower scores. This indicates that existing models still struggle with complex structured output generation tasks.

Different Difficulty Levels

We compare the performance of selected models across three difficulty levels in structured output generation. GPT-4o demonstrates significantly higher proficiency, with a success rate of over 75%, compared to open-source models in the "Easy" category. When it comes to the "Medium" difficulty tasks, while the gap narrows, GPT-4o still leads at around 60%. The performance gap in the "Hard" category across models indicates that as the complexity of the structure increases, even the most advanced models struggle. This reflects current limitations in handling complex structured outputs that require precise formatting and intricate nested structures.

Generation VS Conversion Tasks

We analyze the performance of models on two primary task paradigms: generation (creating structured outputs from natural language instructions) and conversion (transforming one structured format to another). Results show that most models perform better on generation tasks where they can leverage their instruction-following abilities. Conversion tasks prove more challenging as they require precise understanding of both the input and output format specifications.

Error Analysis

We delve into the analysis of errors by GPT-4o, a pivotal aspect for understanding its operational capabilities and limitations in structured output generation. This analysis serves not only to identify the model's current shortcomings but also to guide future enhancements in its design and training. We meticulously examine 150 randomly sampled error instances from GPT-4o's predictions. These instances are analyzed by expert annotators who identify the root causes of mispredictions based on their knowledge and analysis of the expected outputs.
